5 top tips to improve your verbal reasoning score in 2022/2023

July 3, 2022 by Apply Medic

The verbal reasoning section of the UCAT aims to test your ability to read and interpret text excerpts and answer questions regarding particular conclusions that may be drawn from the text.

These questions require no prior knowledge, with all relevant information being present in the passage of text provided.

This section consists of 11 separate pieces of text, each with 4 associated questions. There are 44 questions in this section, with 21 minutes to answer these. There are a variety of question types in the verbal reasoning section. The first question style assesses critical reasoning by reading the passage then assessing the validity of 4 different statements relating to the question. The other main question type is to decide if a single statement is true (on the basis of the information in the passage, the statement is true), false (on the basis of the information in the passage, the statement is false), or can't tell (you cannot tell from the information in the passage whether the statement is true or false).

Verbal reasoning, decision making, quantitative reasoning and abstract reasoning each result in a score between 300 and 900, giving a combined score between 1200 and 3600.

It is also important to note that there is no negative marking in the UCAT, so do not leave any questions unanswered.


5 top tips to improve your verbal reasoning score for UCAT

1. You do not have to go through each passage in order. Look for the passages that are shorter and/or include true/false/can’t tell questions. Attempting the passages/questions that are relatively more straight forward first will mean that you are less stressed and able to think more clearly.

2. For each passage, quickly scan the beginning and end of each paragraph to assimilate to the general topic of the passage and the sub-topics covered in each paragraph. DO NOT READ THE PASSAGE IN DETAIL as this will waste time.

3. For true/false/can’t tell questions, scan the question, and identify any key words. Scan the passage for any of these key words or phrases. Once you have found the relevant sentence relating to the question, read that sentence and the sentence before/after to gain context. Alternatively, you could speed read the entire paragraph to gain even more context. Then you can select your answer.

4. For the other question types including completing a statement or selecting from a series of statements, the approach is slightly different. Begin by scanning the question and the statements in the answer options. You will then essentially treat each of these answer options in the same way as a true/false/can’t tell question. You can either assess each answer option in sequential order or begin with the statements that you think are more likely to be the answer.

5. The time provided to complete the verbal reasoning section is limited. It is therefore essential to have a clear approach. For some, ensuring that each passage is assessed and answered equally is best. For others, allotting your time to most of the passages then guessing the remaining questions in the final minute may be best. The best thing to do is experiment with different techniques under timed conditions using online question banks to find out what works best for you.
